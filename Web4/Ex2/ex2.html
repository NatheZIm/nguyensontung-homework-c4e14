What Mary Oliver's Critics Don't understand

For America's most beloved pote , paying attention to nature is a springboard to the sacred

By Ruth Franklin

Oliver ises nature as a springboard to the sacred - the beating heart of her work
Illustration by Deanna Halsall

"Mary Oliver is saving my life",Paul Chowder , the title character of Nicholson Baker's noverl " the Anthologist" , scrawlsin the margins of Oliver's "New and Selected Poems , Volume one". A struggling poet , Chowder is suffering form a severe case of writer's block . His girlfriend , with whom he's lived for eight years , has just left him , ostensibly he has been unable to write tge long = overdue introduction to a poetry anthology that he has been putting tohether. For solace and inspriration ,he turns to poets who have been his touchstones - Louise Bogan , Theodore roethke , Sara Teasdale - befire discovering Oliver. In her work , he finds consolation : "I imediately felt more sure of what i was doing".Of her poems , hesays , "They're very simple .And yet each has something"

I don't know exactly what a prayer is
I do know how to pay attention, how to fall down
into the grass , how to kneel down in the grass ,
how to be idle and blessed , how to stroll throught the fields,
which is what i have been doing all day.

The cadences are almost Biblical . "Attention is the beginning of devotion," she urges elsewhere
